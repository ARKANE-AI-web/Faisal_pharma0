First · Clinical & Diagnostic Assessment and the Patient Interview Protocol

The four most common respiratory conditions in the pharmacy are: Common ColdAllergic RhinitisCoughSore Throat. Each has an interview protocol that distinguishes it from the others and determines when to treat and when to refer.

1) Common Cold – Differential Diagnosis

Main symptoms: nasal irritation/discharge/sneezing/congestion, throat irritation or soreness, cough (dry then productive) beginning gradually and mildly, mild fever (in infants and pre-schoolers).

Conditions to rule out when diagnosing the common cold
ConditionDistinguishing features
InfluenzaSudden onset and severe symptoms, fever ≥ 38°C always, headache, myalgia, chills.
Suspected COVID-19Sudden onset of at least one of: fever, cough, or shortness of breath.
Allergic rhinitisRecurrent sneezing and nasal itching, ocular symptoms (redness/itch/tearing), appears on allergen exposure.
SinusitisFacial pain or pressure, nasal obstruction.

2) Allergic Rhinitis – Diagnosis & Classification

Nasal symptoms – early phase: watery rhinorrhea, itching (nose and palate), recurrent sneezing. Late phase: congestion, allergic shiner (bluish swelling around the eye). Ocular symptoms: redness, itching, clear watery discharge.

Classification of Allergic Rhinitis
By frequencyBy severity
Intermittent: < 4 days/week or < 4 weeks/year. Mild: no effect on sleep or daily activities.
Persistent: ≥ 4 days/week and ≥ 4 weeks/year. Moderate–severe: sleep disturbance and/or impaired daily activities.

❓ Key distinguishing questions

  • Timing: symptoms in specific months (spring/summer) ← seasonal intermittent allergy; year-round ← perennial allergy.
  • Family history: asthma/eczema/allergy in a first-degree relative strongly supports an allergic diagnosis.
  • Distinguish congestion (relieved by blowing the nose) from anatomical obstruction that warrants referral.

3) Cough – Diagnosis

Cough classification by duration
Acute (< 3 weeks)Subacute / Chronic (> 3 weeks)
Upper respiratory infection (cold, flu, sinusitis, laryngitis), lower infection (acute bronchitis, pneumonia), asthma attack. Post-nasal drip, chronic bronchitis (smokers), asthma/COPD, gastro-esophageal reflux (GERD), ACE-inhibitor effect, heart failure.
Cough classification by nature

Irritating, does not produce thick sputum; accompanies upper viral infection, the acute phase of asthma, or ACE inhibitors. Produces sputum due to hypersecretion or impaired clearance; the patient needs the ability to cough to clear the mucus.

Key questions: since when? What are the associated symptoms (rhinorrhea, congestion, sore throat, wheeze, shortness of breath, chest pain, fever > 38°C for more than 3 days)?

4) Sore Throat – Diagnosis

Causes: viral (70–90%, e.g. cold and flu), bacterial (Strep / bacterial tonsillitis), other (Candida, post-nasal drip, smoking).

Second · Self-Care & OTC Protocol: Drugs, Supplements, Doses & Counseling

A) Antihistamines

Histamine causes vasodilation, increased permeability, and nerve stimulation, producing: rhinorrhea, itching, sneezing, nasal congestion, and ocular symptoms.

First Generation – Sedating

Crosses the blood–brain barrier causing sedation, with anticholinergic effects: blurred vision, urinary retention, dry mouth and throat. Often used in the common cold for its anticholinergic effect, and in insomnia and motion sickness. Short-acting, so taken several times daily.

⛔ First generation is avoided in:

Glaucoma, benign prostatic hyperplasia (BPH), chronic constipation. Examples: Diphenhydramine, Chlorpheniramine, Dimenhydrinate, Dimethindene, Promethazine.

Second Generation – Less Sedating

More selective for peripheral histamine receptors, so side effects are fewer; it is pumped out of the central nervous system so its sedative effect is reduced. Taken once or twice daily (longer duration), and preferred for allergic rhinitis and skin allergy. Order of sedation likelihood (descending): Cetirizine ← Levocetirizine ← Fexofenadine ← Loratadine ← Desloratadine.

Intranasal Antihistamines

Levocabastine, Azelastine, Olopatadine. An excellent option for rapid relief of nasal allergy symptoms (sneezing, itch, rhinorrhea), first-line for mild intermittent allergy, or as an add-on to oral therapy or intranasal steroid. For adults and children > 6 years: two sprays in each nostril twice daily, up to 3–4 times as needed, for up to 8 weeks.

💬 Counseling points

Prime before first use by pressing 3–4 times until a steady mist appears, and shake before each use. Ideally start treatment 2–3 weeks before hay-fever season.

B) Decongestants

They work by constricting the blood vessels in the nasal passages, reducing swelling and congestion. Pseudoephedrine is the most effective, and oral forms are often combined with an antihistamine and an analgesic.

⚠️ Key warnings

  • Topical intranasal: not used for more than 3–5 days to avoid rebound congestion.
  • Not recommended under 6 years (limited efficacy and increased side effects).
  • Oral forms are avoided in the first trimester of pregnancy; topical may be used when needed.
  • Oral forms are avoided especially in uncontrolled hypertension; saline solution is preferred.

⛔ Contraindications to decongestants

Children < 6 years · pregnancy · cardiovascular disease such as hypertension · glaucoma · benign prostatic hyperplasia (BPH) · diabetes.

C) Cough Preparations

1. Antitussives – for dry cough

Dextromethorphan: reduces the urge to cough by acting on the cough reflex in the brain, for short-term use in acute dry cough associated with upper infection in adults. If symptoms persist > 7 days, stop and refer to a physician.

⛔ Dextromethorphan warning

Requires a prescription (referred to Nahdi clinics). Avoided with serotonin-raising antidepressants due to the risk of serotonin syndrome.

Diphenhydramine (1st gen): useful when a cold is accompanied by post-nasal drip; it dries upper airway secretions and raises the central cough threshold.

Honey: a demulcent that soothes the throat and mucous membranes, reduces the severity and frequency of dry cough, and may outperform cough medicines for improving sleep in children. Contraindicated under 1 year. Adult 10ml three times / child 5ml three times.

2. Mucolytics – for wet cough

They break down the mucus structure making it thinner and less viscous and easier to expel, provided the patient can cough to clear it.

⚠️ Caution

Use with caution in patients with active or previous asthma (possible bronchospasm).

Mucolytics and mucoregulators
ComponentNote & dose
BromhexineA prodrug converted to Ambroxol; adult 5–10ml TID.

3. Expectorants – for wet cough

Guaifenesin: increases secretion volume and decreases its viscosity, easing mucus expulsion and relieving chest congestion. Ivy leaf extract: saponins are the active constituent; they increase watery secretions, relax bronchial muscles, and reduce the frequency of respiratory infections. Thyme: thymol and its oils are mucolytic, expectorant, anti-inflammatory, and bronchodilating, and are often combined with ivy for synergy.

4. Expectorant–Antitussive Combination

For the case in which there is a productive cough with a large amount of mucus that makes the cough ineffective and triggers an exhausting dry cough reflex. The antitussive suppresses the irritating dry cough, and the expectorant increases mucus fluidity so the remaining cough becomes effective at clearing it. Example: Guaifenesin + Dextromethorphan (200/15mg capsule: two capsules every 4–6 hours, maximum 8 capsules daily).

5. Special Cough Preparations (Cistus / Cold & Cough)

They target the underlying cause (cold and bronchitis) and the overall symptoms of acute respiratory infection (cough, sore throat, congestion, fatigue). Cistus incanus: long-chain polyphenols coat the virus and prevent its attachment to the mucous membrane, inhibiting its replication, and are often combined with honey to increase contact time.

D) Intranasal Steroids

The most effective single pharmacologic therapy for allergic rhinitis, especially persistent or moderate–severe cases. Onset within hours but maximum effect within days to weeks. Used regularly for the optimal result, started at the highest age-appropriate dose then reduced once controlled. When there is no response, another class is added rather than switching between steroids. Nasal irrigation with saline before use improves efficacy.

Examples of nasal steroids
ComponentCommon dose (>12 yrs)
Triamcinolone / Fluticasone PropionateTwo sprays in each nostril once daily

🤰 In pregnancy

Budesonide is among the nasal steroids that may be used in pregnancy. Fluticasone is likely acceptable. Mometasone has limited systemic absorption and its use is acceptable, but others are supported by more studies and are preferred. Combine with a 2nd-generation antihistamine when needed, at the lowest effective dose.

👃 Correct nasal-spray technique

  1. Shake the bottle (suspension formulation) and prime the pump for a new bottle or after days of non-use.
  2. Tilt the head slightly forward so the drug stays in the nose.
  3. Point the nozzle away from the nasal septum toward the outer wall to reduce irritation and nosebleeds.
  4. Press while gently sniffing at the same time (Spray & Sniff).
  5. Do not blow the nose for 10–15 minutes after spraying. Trick: use the right hand for the left nostril and vice versa.

Steroid + intranasal antihistamine combination: an add-on in moderate–severe cases when the steroid alone is insufficient, e.g. Fluticasone + Azelastine, Mometasone + Olopatadine (two sprays in each nostril twice daily for adults).

E) Nasal Saline

Types of solution by concentration
Isotonic (0.9%)Hypertonic (~2–3%)
At body concentration, for all ages including infants, for moisturizing and cleansing, used as needed. Draws water from swollen nasal tissues by osmosis, reducing congestion (a natural decongestant effect).

Delivery systems: nasal spray (metered, giving a fixed amount, or jet, a continuous stream), drops (preferred for infants for gentleness), and nasal rinse (a squeeze bottle for a continuous cleansing stream). Nasal emollients/moisturizers (gel/ointment/oils such as GeloSitin) moisturize and remove crusts.

🚿 Nasal-rinse method

Use distilled or previously boiled water, fill 120–240ml, shake until the salt dissolves, tilt the head forward, place the nozzle in one nostril and squeeze gently so half the solution exits the other nostril.

F) Throat Demulcents / Lozenges

The most common for the throat; viscous substances that form a protective layer over inflamed membranes, and contain properties: antiseptic (Chlorhexidine, Hydroxy resorcinol, Amylmetacresol, Dichlorobenzyl alcohol), local anesthetic (Benzocaine, Hexylresorcinol), protective layer (Manuka honey, Propolis). Example: Chlorhexidine (adult one lozenge TID; 6–12 yrs ½ lozenge BID).

Third · Critical Cases & Referral Criteria (Red Flags / Referral)

In the following cases, the pharmacist stops dispensing OTC and directs the patient to see a physician immediately.

🚩 Common cold – referral signs

  • Acute sinus pain not responding to a decongestant (acute sinusitis).
  • Ear pain not responding to an analgesic (acute otitis media).
  • Sore throat: tonsillar exudate with high fever and cervical node enlargement; or persistent > 2 weeks; or true painful dysphagia; or recurrent long-standing hoarseness/loss of voice.
  • Cough: persistent > 2 weeks or rapidly worsening; colored sputum (yellow/green/brown) ← possible bacterial infection; worse at night with wheeze in children ← possible asthma.

🚩 Allergic rhinitis – referral signs

  • No improvement after a week of OTC treatment.
  • Ear pain or facial pain (possible sinusitis or otitis media).
  • Purulent mucus for several days (possible infection).
  • Anatomical nasal obstruction not relieved by blowing the nose ← warrants referral.

🚩 Cough – referral signs

  • Duration > 3 weeks or worsening after a short period (3–5 days).
  • Wheeze and/or shortness of breath.
  • Fever ≥ 38°C persisting more than 3 days.
  • Chest pain or pain on inspiration.
  • Colored sputum (yellow/green/brown) ← possible bacterial; or pink frothy sputum with shortness of breath in the elderly ← possible heart failure.

🚩 Sore throat – referral signs

  • Tonsillar exudate with high fever and cervical lymph node enlargement.
  • Recurrent/long-standing hoarseness or loss of voice.
  • True painful dysphagia.
  • Ear pain not resolving after 72 hours or accompanied by discharge.
  • Duration more than two weeks.

👥 Sensitive groups requiring special caution

  • Children 1–6 years: avoid systemic decongestants and the first generation; use Desloratadine/Dimethindene for rhinorrhea, saline + aspirator for congestion, Paracetamol/Ibuprofen for pain and fever, honey (>1 year) and herbal preparations for cough.
  • Chronic-disease patients (hypertension, cardiac, BPH, glaucoma): avoid systemic decongestants (Pseudoephedrine) in hypertension and cardiac disease, and avoid the first generation (Diphenhydramine) in BPH and glaucoma; rely on saline, cooling ointments, an analgesic (Paracetamol), and immunity support.
  • Pregnant women: avoid oral decongestants (first trimester); intranasal Budesonide or Fluticasone are acceptable nasal options.
Fourth · Asthma Management & Medication Adherence

📊 The adherence and awareness gap

  • At least 50% of adults and children do not take the controller medication as prescribed (GINA 2020).
  • Up to 80% of patients do not use the inhaler device correctly.
  • Asthma prevalence in Saudi children is estimated between 8% and 25%; and about half of pregnant women intend to stop asthma medication during pregnancy.

Medication Therapy Management (MTM) & Medication-Related Problems (MRPs)

A medication-related problem (MRP): any undesirable event related, or suspected to be related, to drug therapy that hinders achieving therapy goals. Types: need for additional drug therapy, unnecessary drug, ineffective drug, dose too low, dose too high, adverse drug reaction. The MTM service enables identifying and resolving these problems, thereby reducing costs and improving outcomes.

Core elements of MTM: Medication Therapy Review (MTR), Personal Medication Record (PMR), Medication Action Plan (MAP), Intervention/Referral (I&R), Documentation and follow-up (DOC).

Definition, symptoms, and triggers

Asthma is a common chronic disease characterized by airway inflammation and narrowing (bronchospasm); in genetically predisposed individuals the airways become inflamed and hyper-responsive on exposure to triggers, causing bronchospasm, mucous plugs, and obstruction. Common symptoms: wheeze, shortness of breath, chest tightness, cough.

✅ Easy rule: asthma is controlled if

Daytime symptoms, reliever (SABA) use, and nocturnal awakening are each below the threshold (symptoms/SABA ≤ twice weekly, rare nocturnal awakening). The common complication: acute exacerbations, i.e. a gradual increase in shortness of breath, cough, wheeze, and chest tightness with a fall in expiratory flow.

Medications

⛔ A fundamental GINA update

SABA alone is no longer recommended at step one; it relieves symptoms temporarily but does not protect against severe attacks, and overuse (≥ 3 canisters/year) increases the risk of a severe attack. All adults and adolescents should receive ICS-containing treatment to reduce the risk of serious attacks (either daily, or as-needed low-dose ICS-Formoterol in mild asthma).

Management goals: optimal symptom control at the lowest dose with rare reliever use (<2 days/week), and reducing the risk of attacks, death, and side effects, via a stepwise approach and a continuous cycle: assess ← adjust ← review response.

Inhalation Devices

Correct technique ← better symptom control; so technique is checked at every opportunity.

Patient education and asthma referral criteria

🎓 Key education messages

  • The disease: explain what happens to the airways during an attack, and how to recognize and avoid triggers ("trigger diary").
  • The medication: clarify the difference between the controller (maintenance) and the fast reliever, emphasize using the reliever for sudden symptoms, and ask the patient to show you which device is the reliever to confirm (teach-back).
  • The skill: ask them to demonstrate use of the device and spacer, and assess their ability to monitor control (Rule of Two).
Asthma referral levels

2. Physiology of Pain (the inflammatory pathway)

Tissue damage ← phospholipid ← arachidonic acid. The pathway divides:

PathwayEnzyme/mediatorFunctionInhibitors
LipoxygenaseLeukotrienesBronchoconstrictionLipox inhibitors
COX-1 (protective)Protective prostaglandin + thromboxaneGastric mucosal protection, renal blood flow, platelet aggregationNSAIDs, Aspirin
COX-2 (inflammatory)Inflammatory prostaglandinsPain, inflammation, feverNSAIDs, COX-2 inhibitors, Aspirin
Key point: inhibition of COX-1 is the cause of the gastric, renal, and platelet side effects; whereas inhibition of COX-2 is responsible for the anti-pain, anti-inflammatory, and antipyretic effect.

RAAS system: angiotensinogen ← (Renin) angiotensin I ← (ACE) angiotensin II ← aldosterone; it causes vasoconstriction, thirst, Na+ reabsorption and water retention, and K+ excretion — which explains the effect of NSAIDs on blood pressure and fluid retention.

Precautions when prescribing NSAIDs

  • Use the lowest effective dose for the shortest possible duration.
  • Asthmatic patients (may be prescribed for those with no history of NSAID-related symptoms).
  • Uncontrolled hypertension, peptic ulcer, glaucoma, pregnancy, lactation (except Ibuprofen).
  • Bleeding, renal problems and chronic renal insufficiency, liver problems (especially Diclofenac).
  • Risk of cardiovascular thrombotic events, stroke, and edema (selective COX-2 inhibitors).
  • Cross-sensitivity with sulfonamide, especially Celecoxib.

Key non-selective drugs

Ibuprofen

The anti-inflammatory effect needs a higher dose and a longer time (weeks) than the antipyretic and analgesic effect.

  • Up to 1200 mg/day ← good analgesic and antipyretic effect.
  • 1200–3200 mg/day ← anti-inflammatory effect.
  • Considered the least gastric-irritating, and the only one compatible with lactation.

Aspirin (ASA)

All NSAIDs have a reversible effect on platelet aggregation except ASA, whose effect is irreversible.

  • Used once daily as an antiplatelet (75–100 mg) to prevent strokes and heart attacks.
  • Contraindicated in those under 16 with a viral infection (Reye's syndrome).

Naproxen / Mefenamic acid / Piroxicam

  • Naproxen: the safest in cardiovascular problems.
  • Mefenamic acid: the best option in dysmenorrhea (reduces contraction and dilates vessels in the endometrial tissue).
  • Piroxicam: a very long duration of action (< 36 hours), dose once daily.

Diclofenac

  • Causes elevated liver enzymes and increased risk of hepatotoxicity compared with others.
  • Diclofenac potassium has a faster onset than Diclofenac sodium but a shorter duration.

Selective COX-2 inhibitors

Etoricoxib (Arcoxia)

  • In acute gout pain ← the 120 strength is preferred.
  • In chronic rheumatoid and osteoarthritis ← the 60 or 90 strength is preferred.

Celecoxib (Celebrex)

A selective COX-2 inhibitor; caution regarding cross-sensitivity with sulfonamide.

Paracetamol (Acetaminophen) — a central COX-3 inhibitor

  • Main indication: analgesic and antipyretic only.
  • Maximum dose: 4 g/day; and 2 g/day for the liver patient.
  • Side effect: hepatotoxicity (dose-dependent).
  • Safe in: pregnancy and lactation, the ulcer patient, the cardiac and renal patient.

6. Headache & Migraine

Types of headache

TypeLocation/nature of pain
SinusBehind the brow bone and/or cheeks
TensionLike a band squeezing the head

ClusterAround and inside one eye

Classification: primary (90% — not related to an underlying condition and not life-threatening: migraine, cluster, tension) and secondary (10% — a sign of an underlying condition such as a tumor, trauma, or seizures, and may be life-threatening).

🚩 Referral symptoms (the SNOOP scheme)

  • S — Systemic symptoms/signs (fever, weight loss, fatigue, HIV, cancer, immunodeficiency).
  • N — Neurological symptoms/signs (altered consciousness, focal deficit, confusion).
  • O — Onset sudden (the worst headache of the patient's life).
  • O — Older: new and progressive onset, especially over 50 (temporal arteritis).
  • P — Progressive/positional/prior headache different in frequency or severity, papilledema of the optic nerve.

Additional referrals: a very severe headache, after head injury (traumatic), affected vision, a child under 12 if it recurs, a headache with nausea/vomiting (except diagnosed migraine).

Migraine triggers

Gluten, aged cheese, cold foods and drinks, chocolate.

Treatment

Headache treatment

Analgesics (NSAIDs) or (Paracetamol ± caffeine ± codeine — such as Solpadeine).

Rebound headache: some drugs cause headache (HRT, nitrates, Sildenafil, caffeine, many antibiotics, blood-pressure drugs, and overuse of headache analgesics). To reduce it: take the drug before sleep, lower the dose or use alternatives, rise slowly, be cautious about driving.

Specific migraine treatment — Triptans

Agonists of 5HT1D and 5HT1B receptors: they constrict the cerebral arteries and counteract their dilation, and inhibit the release of the vasodilating peptide (especially CGRP). Examples: Eletriptan (Relpax), Sumatriptan (Imigran).

  • The dose is one tablet as soon as possible after the attack begins; a second dose may be taken after two hours if it returns or a partial response is noted.
  • Maximum dose two tablets within 24 hours, and not used more than two days weekly to reduce rebound headache.

Prevention — CGRP inhibitors (CGRPIs)

They prevent CGRP from binding to its receptors. Examples: Erenumab, Galcanezumab. Recommended for someone with 6 migraine days monthly or failure of older preventive treatments; given as a monthly subcutaneous injection, showing a ≥50% reduction in monthly migraine days.

Complementary migraine therapy

Coenzyme Q10 at 100 mg three times daily for 3 months — reduces headache frequency ~30% and associated nausea days ~45% in adults.

7. Low Back Pain

Causes

  • Soft-tissue injury or overuse (sprain/strain of ligaments and muscles, fractures).
  • Pressure on nerve roots (pain radiating below the knee is more likely neurological; numbness/paresthesia/weakness in the leg or foot).

9. Osteoarthritis (OA)

OA is the most common cause of chronic joint pain; it occurs through cartilage erosion ← pain and stiffness. Before age 55 it affects men more, and after that women more. Its classification: primary (age-related) and secondary (early, after a specific cause such as trauma or obesity).

  • Trauma (car accident or fall).
  • Disability or loss of movement, especially sudden.
  • Pain not responding to common analgesics.
  • Signs of infection: fever, redness, joint swelling.
  • Inflammation, swelling, fever, and pain at rest (rheumatoid/gout/septic arthritis).

10. Gout & Hyperuricemia

Hyperuricemia: increased uric acid in the blood. Gout: attacks of pain, redness, and swelling in the joints from deposition of uric acid crystals (especially the big toe, knee, and ankle). The normal level is < 7.0 mg/dL.

The pathway: RNA/DNA ← purines ← hypoxanthine ← (Xanthine Oxidase) xanthine ← (Xanthine Oxidase) uric acid (poorly water-soluble).

Causes of hyperuricemia

OverproductionUnderexcretion
Purine-rich foods (organ meats, meats, seafood, dairy), xanthine drinks (coffee/tea), certain chemotherapy drugs, hereditary Lesch-Nyhan syndromeCertain drugs (Aspirin, thiazide and loop diuretics), renal insufficiency

Lines of treatment

Anti-inflammatories (for the acute phase)

  • NSAIDs (except Aspirin).
  • Colchicine: within 36 hours. Effects: diarrhea and bloody diarrhea, alopecia, marrow suppression, neuro-myopathy. Another use: Mediterranean fever.
  • Anti-edematous agents: Aescin (Reparil), horse chestnut.

Xanthine oxidase inhibitors (for chronic gout — not used in the acute phase)

  • Allopurinol: a competitive inhibitor.
  • Febuxostat: a non-competitive inhibitor. The dose is increased according to severity.

Drugs that increase uric acid excretion and dissolve crystals

Piperazine, Potassium Sodium Hydrogen Citrate — they stabilize urine pH within the correct range of 6.2–6.8. The dose: early morning one spoon, midday (2–3 pm) one spoon, late evening two spoons (with a color chart to adjust the dose).

Gout patient education

  • Avoid a purine-rich diet (red meat, dairy, beans) and xanthine drinks (black tea, Pepsi, coffee).
  • Beware of certain drugs: diuretics, ACEI, ampicillin, aspirin at a usual analgesic dose, methotrexate, pentoxifylline.
  • Exercise (150–300 minutes/week moderate), weight control (BMI < 25), drink at least 2 liters of water daily to prevent kidney stones.

11. Summary of Professional Counseling Tips

Points that distinguish the pharmacist

  • NSAIDs at the lowest effective dose for the shortest duration; with food to reduce gastric irritation.
  • Paracetamol maximum 4 g/day (2 g for the liver patient) and it is the most suitable for pregnancy, ulcer, cardiac, and renal patients.
  • Ibuprofen is the only one compatible with lactation and the least gastric-irritating.
  • Aspirin is contraindicated under 16 with a viral infection (Reye's syndrome).
  • Naproxen is the safest cardiac-wise, and Mefenamic acid is best for dysmenorrhea.
  • Triptans are not used more than two days weekly to avoid rebound headache.
  • Ankle sprain: RICE and avoid massage in the acute phase, and heat after a day or two.
  • Glucosamine + Chondroitin is stopped if no improvement is noted after 3 months.
  • Xanthine oxidase inhibitors are not started during an acute gout attack.

2. Antimicrobial Resistance (AMR)

What is AMR? It occurs when bacteria, viruses, fungi, and parasites change over time and no longer respond to drugs, making infections harder to treat and increasing the risk of disease spread, severe illness, and death.

Why does it matter? Antibiotics are the cornerstone of modern medicine; without them, the successes of major surgeries and cancer chemotherapy are affected, and we may return to a pre-antibiotic era.

Your role as a pharmacist — the antibiotic guardian

  • Strict adherence: never dispense antibiotics without a valid prescription.
  • Patient education: complete the full course and do not share the drug with others.
  • Viral versus bacterial: antibiotics do not treat influenza (viruses).

4. Critical Cases & Referral Criteria (Referral / Red Flags) 🚩

When the pharmacist stops dispensing OTC entirely and refers the patient to a physician immediately:

🫁 Respiratory infection

  • Persistent fever: a high temperature lasting > 48 hours.
  • Bacterial signs: colored sputum (yellow/green/brown).
  • Severe pain: acute sinus or ear pain not responding to OTC analgesics/decongestants.
  • Chronic symptoms: cough > 2 weeks or worsening (wheeze in children).

🍽️ Gastrointestinal infection

  • Signs of dysentery: blood or mucus in the stool.
  • Systemic symptoms: diarrhea with severe vomiting or persistent fever.
  • Duration: > 4 days for adults or > 2–3 days for children/elderly without improvement.
  • Dehydration: dry mouth, sunken eyes, loss of skin turgor (urgent referral).

🚽 Urinary tract infection (UTI)

  • Systemic signs (upper UTI): high fever, chills, nausea/vomiting, flank pain.
  • High-risk groups: pregnant women and the elderly.
  • Blood in the urine (hematuria).
  • Recurrence: repeated infection or previous treatment failure.

🩹 Skin infection

  • Signs of cellulitis: spreading redness, warmth, swelling, deep tenderness.
  • Systemic involvement: fever, lymph node swelling, a general feeling of malaise.
  • High-risk patients: diabetics (especially foot infection) or the immunodeficient.

6. Professional Patient Counseling

Points that distinguish the professional pharmacist

  • Completing the course: complete the antibiotic fully even with improvement to prevent resistance and relapse.
  • Food interactions: Doxycycline, quinolones, and Cefdinir are separated from dairy/iron/antacids (2–3 hours).
  • Photosensitivity: with tetracyclines — sunscreen and reduced exposure.
  • Body position: Doxycycline and Clindamycin — a full glass of water and remaining upright for 30 minutes.
  • Discoloration of secretions: reassure the patient that Nitrofurantoin colors the urine and Cefdinir+iron colors the stool.
  • Statin interaction: review the patient's medication list before dispensing Clarithromycin.
  • Severe diarrhea: with Clindamycin, stop immediately and seek care if it is watery/bloody.
  • Non-interchangeable strengths: Augmentin at different strengths is not substituted without the physician's approval.

1. Clinical Assessment and Warning Signs

Possible warning signs of diabetes

Unexplained weight loss/gain, frequent urination, severe fatigue, blurred vision, numbness of the hands, constant hunger, increased thirst, slow wound healing.

Normal blood glucose ≈ < 115 mg/dL (HbA1c < 5.7%).

Brief pathophysiology

When glucose is misused: ↓ insulin level ← the liver does not stop producing glucose ← elevated blood glucose ← diabetes (cardiovascular, neurological, and renal complications).

Dose: 3 times daily before meals. Effects: hypoglycemia, weight gain.

Mechanism: an insulin sensitizer, reduces glucose and insulin resistance; the first-line for ADA in type 2. Also effective in polycystic ovary syndrome.

Mechanism: an insulin sensitizer; second/third-line for ADA. Effective in polycystic ovaries.

4. Insulin (Injectable — Type 1 and Type 2)

Insulin is a peptide hormone of two chains; two kinds: human (recombinant DNA technology) and analogs (amino acid sequence modification). Adverse reactions: hypoglycemia, weight gain, lipodystrophy.

Insulin injection sites and technique

  • Inject into the subcutaneous fat layer, not into the muscle.
  • Rotate injection sites with each dose to reduce lipodystrophy.
  • The abdomen (except a 2-inch circle around the navel), the upper/outer thigh (avoid near the knee), the outer-posterior upper arm (fatty tissue).

Insulin storage conditions

  • Record the opening date on the vial.
  • Unopened: in the refrigerator (2–8°C) until the expiry date; opened: room temperature or the refrigerator (injecting cold insulin may be painful).
  • Avoid excessive shaking; discard at expiry or after 28 days of opening or when there is sediment.
  • Cooling solutions: the VIVI Cap cap, the Diabetica travel bag (keeps it cold for 12 hours).

The i-Port Advance device

An injection port that allows taking the drug without pricking the skin for each dose; 5–8 mm needles; worn for up to 3 days (including sports, sleep, and bathing).

6. Critical Cases & Referral Criteria (Red Flags) 🚩

Complications warranting attention and referral

  • Risk of peripheral arterial disease.
  • Neuropathy.
  • Increased risk of infection.
  • Foot problems and the risk of infection, gangrene, and amputation.

The Diabetic Foot

  • The neuropathic foot: warm, dry skin, reduced sensation, callused tissue around the ulcer.
  • The neuro-ischemic foot: cold and pink, atrophic skin, absent pulse, may be painful.

7. Lifestyle, Supplements, and Foot Care

Benefits of a healthy lifestyle

Exercise increases the body's insulin sensitivity and improves blood glucose and mood, and facilitates control.

6 habits for blood glucose control

  • Cleaning up the diet: reducing sugar, choosing non-starchy vegetables and whole grains.
  • Losing 5–7% of body weight improves insulin resistance.
  • Managing stress (stress hormones worsen resistance).
  • Regular movement.
  • Good sleep.
  • Support from family, friends, and the specialist.

Exercise recommendations (Diabetes UK)

Adults: ≥150 minutes weekly; children: one hour daily.

Recommended diets

The Mediterranean diet, and the DASH diet (vegetables, fruits, whole grains, olive oil, legumes, and nuts).

9. Professional Patient Counseling

Points that distinguish the pharmacist

  • Metformin: with food to reduce GI symptoms, follow up on B12 with long-term use, and avoid it in renal insufficiency (lactic acidosis).

HYPERTENSION

3. Hypertension

Pathophysiology

Blood pressure = Cardiac Output × Peripheral Resistance, regulated by the nervous system, hormones, and the kidneys.

  • Sympathetic overactivity: excessive noradrenaline release → vasoconstriction + increased heart rate + vascular wall remodeling → sustained pressure elevation.
  • RAAS overactivity: the kidneys secrete renin → angiotensin I → angiotensin II (a potent vasoconstrictor) → stimulates aldosterone → sodium and water retention → increased blood volume.
  • Renal sodium retention: blood volume expansion for genetic or structural reasons.

Definition and classification

Definition: a sustained systolic pressure ≥ 140 mmHg or a sustained diastolic pressure ≥ 90 mmHg.

Hypertensive crisis: SBP > 180 and/or DBP > 120 mmHg.

ClassificationSystolic (mmHg)RelationDiastolic (mmHg)
Normal< 120and< 80

Hypertension> 140or> 90

Classified based on at least two readings on at least two separate occasions. When the systolic and diastolic categories differ, the higher category is chosen.

SecondaryLess common 5–10% — the result of renal or endocrine diseases

Complications: retinopathy, renal failure, heart failure, peripheral vascular disease, left ventricular hypertrophy (LVH), coronary artery disease.

Practical tips

  • Thiazides are taken in the morning to avoid nocturnal urination.
  • Caution with a history of acute gout unless the patient is on uric-acid-lowering therapy.
  • ACEIs/ARBs: avoid in pregnancy; cough due to bradykinin (with ACE only); do not combine ACE with ARB in the same patient.
  • Dihydropyridine CCBs cause more reflex tachycardia and peripheral edema; non-dihydropyridines slow the heart and may cause block — avoid them with beta-blockers.
  • Different release mechanisms among extended-release preparations — beware when switching between one product and another.

Fixed-dose combinations

  • ARB + Amlodipine: valsartan/olmesartan/irbesartan/telmisartan/candesartan + amlodipine.
  • ARB + HCTZ: valsartan/irbesartan/candesartan/olmesartan/telmisartan + hydrochlorothiazide.
  • Triple: valsartan or olmesartan + amlodipine + hydrochlorothiazide.
  • Others: bisoprolol + amlodipine, bisoprolol + hydrochlorothiazide.

Healthy lifestyle and follow-up

  • DASH diet: abundant in fruits, vegetables, whole grains, nuts, legumes, and low-fat dairy; reduces salt (no more than 3.8 g NaCl daily), red meat, and sweets. Encourages potassium, magnesium, and calcium.
  • Weight loss: every 10 kg lost → a systolic reduction of 5–20 mmHg; the target BMI is between 18.5–24.9.
  • Physical activity: 3–4 sessions weekly, ~40 minutes → a systolic reduction of 4–9 mmHg.
  • Smoking cessation: the pressure begins to normalize within days, and circulation improves. (Nicorette).

Home blood pressure measurement — how to use it

  • Rest for 5 minutes before measuring, back supported, the arm on a table, bare and supported.
  • The middle of the cuff at heart level, the lower edge 3 cm above the elbow crease.
  • Empty the bladder, do not talk or move, avoid caffeine and smoking, do not cross the legs, feet flat on the floor.
  • Device-selection criteria: type (arm/wrist), cuff size, blood-pressure reading range, irregular-pulse detection, multiple users. (Examples: Yuwell, B.well Pro-35/Med-55, Huawei D2 Smartwatch).

Laboratory and medical follow-up

Physician follow-up every 3–6 months (and monthly if the pressure is uncontrolled) to review control, adjust medications, assess side effects, and monitor complications.

Periodic tests: CBC, fasting/HbA1c, a fasting lipid profile, creatinine and eGFR, electrolytes (Na, K — especially with diuretics/ACE/ARB), thyroid function TSH, urinalysis, the urine albumin/creatinine ratio (ACR) for early detection of kidney damage.

ANGINA

4. Angina

Definition and symptoms

Angina is pain or discomfort in the chest that occurs when the heart muscle does not get enough oxygen-rich blood, often due to narrowing of the coronary arteries as a result of atherosclerosis.

  • Pressure, tightness, or squeezing in the chest, may radiate to the arm, shoulder, neck, jaw, or back.
  • Women are more prone to other symptoms: shortness of breath, nausea, back or jaw pain.
  • Often provoked by physical activity or stress, and improves with rest or nitroglycerin.
TypeCharacteristics
Stable anginaChest discomfort provoked by activity, resolving with rest or after nitroglycerin.
Unstable anginaOccurs even at rest or lying down or with little effort; the pain is worse and lasts longer; may be accompanied by shortness of breath — a warning sign.

Angina is a warning sign of coronary artery disease (CAD) and may progress to a heart attack if not managed correctly. Complications: myocardial infarction, reduced quality of life, sudden cardiac death.

Medications

The primary goal of treatment: reducing the heart's work and thereby its oxygen demand.

Drugs for angina complications (heart failure / arrhythmia)

  • Digoxin (a cardiac glycoside): strengthens cardiac contraction and regulates rhythm; used in heart failure and atrial fibrillation, often with a diuretic + ACE + beta-blocker.
  • Sacubitril (ARNI): the first neprilysin inhibitor; approved for chronic heart failure. Because neprilysin breaks down angiotensin II, it is not used alone and must be combined with an ARB (valsartan).
  • Amiodarone: one of the most used antiarrhythmics, a very long half-life (weeks), may take 6 weeks for full effect; rich in iodine so it affects the thyroid.

Education, supplements, and follow-up

  • Adherence: always carry nitroglycerin if prescribed and use it at the first sign of chest pain; do not skip doses.
  • Complementary supplement — L-Carnitine: may improve exercise tolerance and angina symptoms during exercise — 900 mg–2 g daily for 6 months. Avoid with warfarin.
  • Lifestyle: avoid triggers (heavy exertion, stress, smoking), a heart-healthy diet, a healthy weight, and safe activity.

When to seek emergency help immediately

  • The pain lasts more than 10 minutes, or occurs at rest, or differs from usual.
  • Accompanied by shortness of breath, sweating, nausea, or fainting.
THROMBOSIS

5. Thrombosis

Definition and mechanism

Thrombosis is the formation of a blood clot inside a vessel or a chamber of the heart. The clot may block blood flow or detach and travel to another location.

Aspect (Virchow's triad)DescriptionCauses
1. Endothelial injuryVessel damage stimulates platelet activation and initiation of clottingHypertension, smoking, diabetes, surgery, trauma
2. Abnormal blood flowSlow/turbulent flow allows accumulation of platelets and clotting factorsProlonged sitting, immobility, varicose veins
3. HypercoagulabilityThe blood is "over-primed" to clotGenetic disorders, cancer, pregnancy, hormonal therapy

The clotting cascade: Factor Xa → thrombin (IIa) → converts fibrinogen to fibrin → a mesh trapping platelets and cells → a stable solid clot.

Symptoms of venous thromboembolism (VTE)

Pain and swelling in one leg, tenderness, redness, increased leg warmth, deep pain in the leg or thigh, tachycardia. Complications: deep vein thrombosis (DVT), mesenteric ischemia.

7. Critical Cases & Referral Criteria (Red Flags)

Stop dispensing OTC and refer to a physician immediately when:

  • Hypertensive crisis: SBP > 180 and/or DBP > 120 mmHg.
  • Unstable angina / cardiac emergency: chest pain lasting > 10 minutes, or at rest, or different from usual, or accompanied by shortness of breath, sweating, nausea, or fainting.
  • VTE/DVT symptoms: pain and swelling in one leg with redness, tenderness, and local warmth.
  • Uncontrolled pressure despite treatment: monthly follow-up and referral to adjust the drug regimen.
  • Uncontrolled dyslipidemia: LDL above target despite maximum statin + ezetimibe → referral for specialty drugs.

Sensitive groups — always beware

  • Pregnancy: absolutely prohibited — ACEIs, ARBs, statins, warfarin, and many supplements (berberine, algae, hibiscus). The safe alternative for pregnancy hypertension: methyldopa.
  • Lactation: prohibited — statins, blue-green algae, hibiscus.
  • Asthma: avoid beta-blockers (β2 blockade).
  • Gout: beware thiazides (hyperuricemia).
  • Blood-thinner/warfarin patients: avoid ginseng, ginkgo, garlic, ginger, omega-3, pomegranate, hibiscus, algae.
  • Before surgery: stop warfarin 5 days, and garlic at least two weeks.

First: Neurological Basics and Neurotransmitters (Foundations)

The central nervous system is the body's command center and consists of the brain and spinal cord, and is responsible for processing information and sending signals to control movement, sensation, memory, mood, and behavior. Nerve cells communicate via chemical messages called neurotransmitters, and any imbalance in them leads to disease.

NeurotransmitterPrimary functionClinical importance
DopamineMovement, motivation, rewardIts deficiency ← Parkinson's / its excess ← schizophrenia and addiction
SerotoninMood, sleep, appetiteIts deficiency is linked to depression
NorepinephrineStress response, attentionA target for antidepressants (SNRIs)
GABAThe main inhibitory transmitter ("the brakes") — calms brain activityEnhancing it ← antiepileptics and hypnotics
GlutamateThe main excitatory transmitter ("the accelerator") — learning and memoryIts overactivity ← epilepsy / its inhibition ← neuroprotection (Memantine)
AcetylcholineMemory and muscle controlIts deficiency ← dementia (Alzheimer)
The three fundamental faults when the command center fails:
  • Structural Decay: death of nerve cells (neurodegeneration) — as in Parkinson's and dementia.
  • Signal Imbalance: abnormally decreased or increased transmitters — as in depression and schizophrenia.
  • Network Chaos: chaotic, uncontrolled signal firing — as in epilepsy.

Second: Interview Protocol and Clinical Assessment within the Pharmacy

Before any recommendation of self-care or supplements in nervous-system conditions, the pharmacist must gather the following essential information:

General questions (for all cases)
  • Who is the medicine for? And what is the age? (special sensitivity for the elderly and children).
  • Since when did the symptoms begin? And are they recurrent or for the first time?
  • Are there chronic diseases (liver, kidney, heart, pregnancy/lactation)?
  • What are the current medications and supplements? (to avoid interactions).
  • Are there warning signs warranting referral? (see the referral section).
Complaint-directed questions
  • Insomnia: difficulty initiating sleep or maintaining it? A shift/travel factor? Caffeine? Do they take beta-blockers?
  • Smoking: how many cigarettes daily? When is the first cigarette after waking? (the HSI indicator).
  • Mood/anxiety: since when? Are there thoughts of self-harm? (immediate referral).
  • Dementia/Parkinson's: who provides care? Extent of medication adherence? Is the home environment safe?

Part One: Neurological Disorders

1) Parkinson's Disease

Clinical overview

A neurodegenerative disease fundamentally associated with the presence of Lewy bodies — abnormal protein aggregates that accumulate inside cells. The primary result is the progressive loss of dopamine-producing cells essential for regulating movement. The most notable signs: tremor, rigidity, bradykinesia, a stooped posture, a masked face, and a shuffling short-stepped gait.

Patient / caregiver education

  • Maintaining balance: keeping the arms free when walking to help with stability.
  • Deliberate movement: changing positions slowly, especially when standing up, and using supports.
  • Preventing fatigue: planned rest periods to conserve energy.
  • Nutritional support: a high-fiber diet to manage constipation (a common non-motor symptom).
  • The importance of regular exercise to maintain movement and balance.

2) Dementia

Clinical overview

A neurodegenerative disease characterized by loss of cholinergic nerve cells, leading to progressive deterioration in memory, thinking, and behavior. The primary problem: reduced acetylcholine. The underlying process is degenerative and often associated with accumulation of proteins such as beta-amyloid. Symptoms: memory loss, difficulty with words and familiar tasks, confusion in time/place, mood swings.

Caregiver education — creating a safe environment and reducing frustration

  • A safe environment: clearing hallways and installing supports; securing dangerous materials and drugs with locks; setting the water heater below 49°C to prevent burns; effective smoke detectors and monitoring cooking; supervision when smoking.
  • Reducing frustration: simple single-step instructions; reducing distractions and noise; offering two specific choices instead of an open question; flexibility, patience, and focusing on the person not the task.

Dietary supplements

Acetyl-L-Carnitine

May slow Alzheimer's progression and improve some measures of cognitive function.

Dose: 1.5–3 g daily for 3–12 months.

Ginkgo Biloba

May improve symptoms of various types of dementia (low doses are not always effective), but does not appear to prevent or slow progression.

Dose: 240 mg daily for 22–24 weeks.

Warning: avoided with anticoagulants.

3) Epilepsy

Clinical overview

A neurological disorder characterized by recurrent, unprovoked seizures resulting from abnormal electrical activity in the brain. The core of the problem: hyper-excitable cells firing uncontrollably and synchronously. At the transmitter level: increased glutamate activity (excitatory) + decreased GABA activity (inhibitory). The goal of treatment is to restore this balance.

Antiepileptic drug (AED) classes — two main mechanisms

Reducing excitation blocking sodium/calcium channels to reduce the cell's likelihood of firing.   Increasing inhibition enhancing the effect of GABA.

Warnings and safety alerts (Cautions)

  • Liver-enzyme inhibitor (slows metabolism and increases concentration and toxicity risk): Valproic Acid.
  • Valproate: possible severe hepatotoxicity.
  • Lamotrigine: a risk of life-threatening rash (SJS) — slow titration.
  • All antiepileptics: increased risk of suicidal thoughts/behavior — monitor mood.

Patient education — adherence, avoiding triggers + first aid for a seizure

Adherence is vital: antiepileptics require stable blood concentrations; missing doses drops the level below the therapeutic threshold and increases the risk of a breakthrough seizure. Common triggers: lack of sleep, stress, illness/fever, flashing lights, alcohol/drugs, and some medications.

First aid for a seizure: do not restrain the patient; gently turn them on their side; time the seizure and call the ambulance if it exceeds 5 minutes; cushion their head and remove glasses; loosen tight clothing around the neck; do not put anything in their mouth; look for a medical bracelet; stay with them until they fully regain consciousness.

Part Two: Psychological Disorders

4) Insomnia

Clinical overview

A persistent disorder characterized by difficulty initiating sleep, maintaining it, or non-restful sleep despite adequate opportunity, leading to clear daytime impairment. An important context: insomnia may be a diagnostic and accompanying symptom of other conditions — it is one of the nine core symptoms of major depressive disorder (MDD), and a hallmark of opioid withdrawal (peaking about 72 hours after the last dose). The imbalance: the "calming" GABA signals are insufficient to overcome the "activating" glutamate signals.

Pharmacotherapy protocol (OTC)

Antihistamines

Mechanism: blocking the H1 histamine receptors in the central nervous system (histamine is a wakefulness-activating transmitter, so blocking it causes sedation).

Main drug: Diphenhydramine.

Use: best for mild, transient, or occasional insomnia; and used to manage withdrawal symptoms (including insomnia) when stopping opioids.

Side effects and essential counseling points

  • Noticeable morning drowsiness (a "hangover" effect).
  • Strong anticholinergic effects: dry mouth, blurred vision, constipation, urinary retention.
  • High risk in the elderly: may cause confusion and delirium and increase the risk of falling. (Likewise Nortriptyline, with its high anticholinergic effect, is avoided in the elderly).

Other drug classes mentioned: GABA-A receptor agonists ("Z-Drugs") directly enhance the "off switch," melatonin receptor agonists regulate the natural sleep-wake cycle, and other drugs with sedative properties used off-label for patients with comorbidities such as depression.

Patient education — sleep hygiene

  • A consistent daily sleep schedule (even on holidays) to set the biological clock.
  • Preparing the sleep environment: a dark, quiet, cool, and comfortable room.
  • A relaxation routine one hour before sleep (reading, calm music, light stretching).
  • Avoiding stimulants: caffeine, nicotine, and alcohol, especially before sleep.
  • The bed for sleep only (avoid working, eating, and watching TV in it).

5) Smoking Addiction

Clinical overview

Smoking is not a lifestyle choice but a chronic relapsing disease; nicotine is a potent addictive substance that reshapes the brain. Globally, tobacco kills more than 8 million people annually (WHO). How nicotine hijacks the brain: a trigger (coffee/stress) ← nicotine reaches the brain within seconds and releases a flood of dopamine (the "feel-good chemical") in the nucleus accumbens ← reward and reinforcement that entrench the addictive loop (Habit Loop).

Assessment tool — the Heavy Smoking Index (HSI)

How many cigarettes do you smoke daily? ≤10 (0), 11–20 (1), 21–30 (2), ≥31 (3).
When is the first cigarette after waking? within 5 minutes (3), 6–30 minutes (2), 31–60 minutes (1), after 60 minutes (0).

Interpretation: an HSI total ≥ 4 indicates a high level of nicotine dependence.

  • The timeline of quitting benefits: 20 minutes (pulse and pressure drop) ← 12 hours (carbon monoxide returns to normal) ← 1–9 months (lung function improves) ← 1 year (coronary heart risk is halved) ← 2–5 years (stroke risk like a non-smoker) ← 10 years (lung cancer risk half that of an active smoker).
  • Breaking behavioral chains: identify triggers ← change the routine ← replace the reward with a healthier one.
  • The 4 Ds when a craving hits (it passes within 5–10 minutes): Delay, Deep Breathe, Drink Water, Do Something Else.

6) Depression

Clinical overview

A mood disorder that causes a persistent feeling of sadness and loss of interest, affecting feeling, thinking, and behavior. The transmitter problem: reduced serotonin, norepinephrine, and dopamine. Symptoms: low mood, appetite changes, agitation, fatigue, sleep disturbance, difficulty concentrating.

Patient education

  • Patience is key: the therapeutic effect takes 2–4 weeks — manage the patient's expectations.
  • Adherence is critical: do not stop the drug suddenly — abrupt discontinuation causes withdrawal symptoms; gradual tapering is essential.
  • Managing side effects: discuss the initial symptoms (nausea, insomnia) and clarify that they are often transient, with clear instructions on when the patient should see the care provider.

7) Schizophrenia

Clinical overview

A type of chronic psychosis characterized by delusions and hallucinations (often auditory in the form of voices) and disturbances of thinking and speech. Positive symptoms: delusions, hallucinations, disorganized speech. Negative: blunted affect, poverty of speech, loss of initiative. Antipsychotics produce a state of calm without inducing sleep.

Atypical antipsychotics

Mechanism: dopamine antagonism + serotonin-receptor blocking activity.

Drug (brand)Active ingredient
Zyprexa®Olanzapine
Seroquel®Quetiapine
Ridon®Risperidone
Leponex®Clozapine
Therapeutic uses
  • Treatment of schizophrenia.
  • Prevention of nausea and vomiting.
  • Management of manic and mixed symptoms in bipolar disorder.
  • A sedative and antipsychotic effect — such as Dogmatil® (Sulpiride), Solian® (Amisulpride).

Adverse effects

  • Extrapyramidal effects.
  • Tardive dyskinesia.
  • Drowsiness (CNS depression and antihistamine effect).
  • Hypothalamic suppression ← disturbed thermoregulation, amenorrhea, galactorrhea, gynecomastia, infertility, erectile dysfunction.

Fourth: Referral Criteria and Critical Cases (Red Flags) 🚩

When does the pharmacist immediately stop dispensing OTC and refer to a physician?

  • Suicidal thoughts or behavior / self-harm: any sign of it (associated with depression and with all antiepileptics) ← urgent immediate referral.
  • Psychotic symptoms: delusions, hallucinations, disorganized speech ← a possible schizophrenia case requiring a specialist physician (not OTC).
  • A seizure exceeding 5 minutes or a first-time seizure ← call an ambulance immediately.
  • Suspected status epilepticus: a medical emergency.
  • Chronic insomnia or insomnia accompanying severe depression/anxiety or not responding to OTC measures and sleep hygiene ← medical evaluation.
  • New/worsening Parkinson's or dementia symptoms: diagnosis and drug initiation are the physician's responsibility.
  • Signs of drug toxicity: hepatotoxicity (Valproate), a skin rash with Lamotrigine (suspected SJS) ← stop and urgent referral.
  • Severe mood/behavioral symptoms with Varenicline (depression, mood changes) ← see the physician.

Sensitive groups requiring double caution

  • The elderly: avoid strong antihistamines (Diphenhydramine) and Nortriptyline due to the risk of confusion, delirium, and falls.
  • Pregnancy: Ashwagandha is prohibited in pregnancy.
  • Coagulation patients: avoid Ginkgo Biloba with anticoagulants.
  • Epilepsy patients: Bupropion lowers the seizure threshold and is contraindicated for them.
  • Severe kidney patients: Varenicline requires dose adjustment.
  • Enzyme interactions: attention to liver-enzyme inducers/inhibitors when dispensing any accompanying drug.

Fifth: Mock Assessment Question Bank (Practice Exam & Cases)

15 questions simulating community-pharmacy assessments — click each question to reveal the answer and scientific explanation.

Question 1 — A Parkinson's patient complains that Sinemet® is ineffective. What is the most important advice about timing?

Answer: take it on an empty stomach, about 30 minutes before the meal.

Levodopa competes with dietary proteins for absorption; taking it on an empty stomach maximizes absorption and efficacy and a higher concentration reaching the brain.

Question 2 — A patient asks about the reason for brown urine after starting a Parkinson's drug. Your response?

Answer: this is a known and benign side effect of levodopa (change of saliva and urine color to brown), reassure her and not to worry.

Levodopa's side effects also include: gastric upset, visual and auditory hallucinations, and involuntary movements (dyskinesia).

Question 3 — In dementia, which neurotransmitter's deficiency does the primary treatment target, and what is the appropriate class?

Answer: acetylcholine deficiency; and the primary class is acetylcholinesterase inhibitors (Donepezil, Rivastigmine, Galantamine).

They inhibit the enzyme that breaks down acetylcholine, increasing its availability. As for Memantine, it is an NMDA antagonist acting on the glutamate pathway as neuroprotection.

Question 4 — A dementia patient on Donepezil complains of dizziness and fainting. Which cardiac side effect do you suspect?

Answer: bradycardia.

AChE inhibitors may cause bradycardia due to increased cholinergic activity; it warrants medical evaluation.

Question 5 — A customer requests Ginkgo Biloba to improve his father's memory, and mentioned he takes Warfarin. Your decision?

Answer: do not dispense Ginkgo — it is avoided with anticoagulants (Warfarin) due to bleeding risk.

This is an explicit warning interaction; refer to the physician to discuss safe alternatives.

Question 6 — An epilepsy patient started Lamotrigine and developed a spreading skin rash. What is your action?

Answer: urgent referral immediately — suspected life-threatening Stevens-Johnson syndrome (SJS).

Lamotrigine requires slow dose titration specifically to reduce this risk; the rash is a red flag that is not ignored.

Question 7 — Which antiepileptic is a liver-enzyme inhibitor (unlike the others that are inducers)?

Answer: Valproic Acid (an inhibitor).

The inducers: Phenytoin, Carbamazepine, Phenobarbital (accelerate the metabolism of other drugs and reduce their efficacy). The inhibitor slows metabolism and increases concentration and toxicity risk.

Question 8 — Someone comes complaining of recurrent migraine and is a smoker, and mentioned his physician prescribed a drug used for both epilepsy and headache that causes weight loss. What is it?

Answer: Topiramate (Ipramax®).

Multiple mechanisms (Na⁺, GABA, Glutamate), used for migraine prevention, and its symptoms include weight loss, kidney stones, and cognitive slowing ("Dopamax").

Question 9 — Case: a young man who works shifts complains of difficulty initiating sleep. What is the most suitable supplement and why?

Answer: Melatonin.

Shift-worker sleep disorder is caused by circadian rhythm disturbance and melatonin deficiency, and the primary problem is "sleep onset"; melatonin works immediately (30–60 minutes) and reduces sleep-onset time. (Most suitable for DSPS, shift work, jet lag, and the elderly).

Question 10 — Case: a pregnant woman requests Ashwagandha for anxiety and insomnia. Your clinical decision?

Answer: do not dispense — Ashwagandha is prohibited in pregnancy.

Refer to the physician for a safe evaluation of her condition; and non-pharmacologic sleep hygiene measures can be discussed.

Question 11 — Case: an elderly patient requests over-the-counter sleeping pills, and another customer suggested Diphenhydramine for him. Your comment?

Answer: avoid Diphenhydramine in the elderly due to the risk of confusion, delirium, and increased fall risk, in addition to its strong anticholinergic effects.

The most suitable is starting with sleep hygiene; melatonin is a safer option for age-related insomnia (reduced melatonin production), or referral to the physician.

Question 12 — A smoker who smokes 25 cigarettes daily and lights the first cigarette within 5 minutes of waking. What is the HSI score and its significance?

Answer: 25 cigarettes = 2 points + within 5 minutes = 3 points ← total 5 (≥4).

It indicates a high level of nicotine dependence; a high-strength patch protocol is advised (heavy smoker >10): 6–8 weeks high ← two weeks medium ← two weeks low.

Question 13 — A known epilepsy patient requests help quitting smoking and asked about Bupropion (Wellbutrin®). Your decision?

Answer: Bupropion is not used for him — it lowers the seizure threshold and is contraindicated with epilepsy disorders.

The most suitable alternative: nicotine replacement therapy (NRT) available without a prescription, or referral to the physician to discuss Varenicline.

Question 14 — A depression patient very worried about SSRI-associated sexual dysfunction. Which alternative may suit him? And another depressed, underweight patient suffering insomnia?

Answer: for the first, Bupropion (Wellbutrin®) — a useful option for those worried about sexual dysfunction. And for the second, Mirtazapine (Remeron®) — its sedation suits insomnia and increased appetite/weight benefits the underweight.

Employing the side effects clinically is useful according to the patient's profile.

Question 15 — A patient started an SSRI 5 days ago and called angrily because he "feels no improvement" and wants to stop it immediately. Your educational response?

Answer: the therapeutic effect takes 2–4 weeks (patience is key), and the drug is not stopped suddenly because abrupt discontinuation causes withdrawal symptoms — discontinuation is by gradual tapering under the physician's supervision.

Reassure him that the initial symptoms (nausea/insomnia) are often transient, clarifying when to see the care provider.

First: Clinical & Diagnostic Pharmaceutical Assessment

The case-management methodology in the pharmacy is based on three steps: Ask ← Recommend ← Educate. The primary interview tool is the WWHAM protocol.

Distinctive quality questions for the professional pharmacist

  • Nature of stool in diarrhea: watery (often viral) / bloody or mucoid (invasive bacterial infection or IBD) — determines the decision to dispense or refer.
  • Nature of pain in heartburn: does it radiate to other places (may be cardiac CVS or biliary)? Does it occur on an empty stomach (gastric ulcer) or 2–3 hours after eating and wake the patient at night (duodenal ulcer)?
  • Dehydration symptoms: dry mouth and lips, sunken eyes, pallor, reduced urination, thirst — an indicator of severity, especially in children and the elderly.

Second: Heartburn & Dyspepsia (GERD)

Main symptoms: burning sensation, regurgitation, belching — due to relaxation of the lower esophageal sphincter (LES).

Lines of Treatment

1. Antacids rapid relief

  • Components: Sodium bicarbonate, Calcium carbonate, Mg/Al salts.
  • Forms: effervescent, chewable tablets, suspension.
  • Mechanism of action: neutralize stomach acid.
  • Preference: antacids containing alginate such as Gaviscon are better at controlling symptoms and longer-acting (they form a physical barrier over the stomach contents).
  • Dose: as needed, or after meals and at bedtime.

Important drug interaction

They reduce the absorption of other drugs — they must be separated from any other drug by at least two hours.

2. Proton Pump Inhibitors (PPIs) strongest acid inhibition

  • Examples: Omeprazole 20mg, Esomeprazole 20mg, Rabeprazole 10mg, Lansoprazole 15mg, Pantoprazole 20mg, Dexlansoprazole.
  • Mechanism of action: they inhibit 90–98% of acid secretion within 24 hours, and their effect is longer than antacids and H2 blockers.
  • Nature: all are prodrugs and enteric-coated to protect them from stomach acid.
  • Method of use: taken 30–60 minutes before the meal for maximum efficacy (food reduces bioavailability by about 50%).
  • Duration in OTC: heartburn only — not dispensed for more than two weeks, and at a low strength. For moderate–severe symptoms, extended to 8 weeks by prescription only.

Side effects

Headache, dizziness, elevated liver enzymes (Pantoprazole has the least effect), constipation or diarrhea. Long-term: reduced stomach acidity ← reduced absorption of vitamin B12, folate, Mg, Ca, Zinc, Copper, Iron.

Critical drug interactions

  • They reduce the absorption of: Domperidone, Fluconazole, Itraconazole.
  • Omeprazole + Plavix (Clopidogrel): omeprazole inhibits the CYP2C19 enzyme necessary to convert Plavix to its active form ← reduced antiplatelet activity. (An alternative such as Pantoprazole is preferred).

3. H2 histamine receptor blockers (H2 Antagonists)

Examples: Ranitidine, Famotidine, Nizatidine — moderate inhibition of acid secretion, between antacids and PPIs.

4. Prokinetics — Domperidone

  • Usually prescribed with PPIs to treat GERD, raises LES pressure and stimulates gastric motility.
  • Taken 15–30 minutes before the meal. The maximum dose is 30 mg/day for one week.
  • Contraindications: cardiac disorders, hepatic insufficiency, patients over 60 years.

  • Avoid large meals and reduce fats.

  • Raise the head of the bed 10–20 cm (4–8 inches).

Third: Peptic Ulcer

Lines of treatment

  • PPIs: Omeprazole, Esomeprazole, Pantoprazole, Rabeprazole, Lansoprazole, Dexlansoprazole.
  • Eradication of H. pylori (antibiotics): the triple regimen = PPI + Amoxicillin + Clarithromycin (or Metronidazole instead of clarithromycin).

Complementary treatment

Manuka Honey (the active substance methylglyoxal) as a supportive adjunct.

Fourth: Nausea & Vomiting

Anti-Emetics

Dopamine Antagonists

Metoclopramide (Primperan®): a central and peripheral dopamine antagonist, raises LES tone and improves gastric emptying (used in GERD and diabetic gastroparesis). Pregnancy Category B

Side effects: extrapyramidal symptoms (EPS) – involuntary movements, and prolonged use may cause irreversible tardive dyskinesia, in addition to drowsiness.

Domperidone (Motilium®): prescribed with PPIs for GERD, taken 15–30 minutes before the meal, maximum dose 30 mg (one tablet three times) for one week. Contraindications: heart disease, hepatic insufficiency, over 60 years.

5-HT3 antagonists — Ondansetron

  • For prevention of chemotherapy-induced nausea and vomiting (CINV), radiation, surgery, post-operative.
  • Used in morning sickness and hyperemesis of pregnancy Category B, and safe in children.
  • Tablets, syrup, injections.

Anti-histamines — for motion sickness

  • They block H1 and M1 (anticholinergic activity) in the vestibular system.
  • Examples: Diphenhydramine, Dimenhydrinate, Meclizine, Doxylamine, Promethazine — the most effective for preventing motion sickness.
  • Taken 30–60 minutes before travel (two tablets).
  • Meclizine + Pyridoxine (B6) for nausea and vomiting of pregnancy Category B.
Anticholinergic effects: constipation, urinary retention, blurred vision, dry mouth.

Sixth: Constipation

Definition: a reduction in defecation frequency below normal (from 3 times daily to twice weekly) with harder stool and straining on defecation.

Laxatives

1. Bulk-forming laxatives onset 24h–72h

  • Fiber: Ispaghula (Psyllium), soluble and insoluble, sometimes with FOS or spirulina or Ispaghula+Senna.
  • They work within 24 hours but may need 2–3 days for the full effect.
  • Fiber is increased gradually to avoid bloating and cramps, with increased fluids.
  • Dose: a teaspoon (≈5 g) swallowed with a liquid without dissolving, or mixed with 250 ml water/juice.

2. Stimulant laxatives onset 6–12h

  • Bisacodyl | Sodium Picosulfate (minimally absorbed with no systemic effect).
  • Anthraquinones (Senna): a prodrug activated by colonic bacteria (slow onset 10–14 hours), may color the urine yellow-brown to red depending on acidity.
  • Combination: Senna + Docusate (a stool softener).
  • Not intended for long-term use.

Seventh: Irritable Bowel Syndrome (IBS)

Symptoms: abdominal pain, bloating, change in bowel habits. Classification: IBS-D (diarrhea), IBS-C (constipation), IBS-M/A (mixed/alternating).

2. Anti-flatulence — for bloating

Simethicone or Activated Charcoal (3–4 times daily). Activated charcoal reduces the absorption of other drugs and is separated by more than two hours.

3. Treating the predominant symptom

  • IBS-D: Loperamide (initially 2–4 tablets/day, maximum 6–8 tablets/16mg, stop if no improvement within 48 hours).
  • IBS-C: bulk-forming laxatives (soluble fiber such as Ispaghula, Psyllium).

Complementary treatments

  • Soluble fiber is effective for general symptoms and pain, while insoluble is avoided. Psyllium improves both constipation and diarrhea. Started at a low dose and increased slowly over weeks.
  • Probiotics: may benefit general symptoms and pain, without a recommendation of a specific strain, taken for up to 12 weeks (Enterogermina, Lactobacillus+Bifidobacterium).

Eighth: Ulcerative Colitis

A disease that causes inflammation and ulcers in the lining of the large intestine, affecting any age but the majority are diagnosed before age thirty.

Lines of treatment

  • Mesalazine (anti-inflammatory).
  • Budesonide (corticosteroid).

This condition requires diagnosis and medical supervision — the pharmacist's role is supportive and referral.

Ninth: Hemorrhoids

Definition: enlarged/swollen veins in the lower rectum. Classification: internal (4 grades by prolapse), external (very painful), mixed.

Main symptoms: itching around the anus, pain on defecation, blood in the stool (bright red spots on the stool surface or in the toilet).

Treatment

  • Oral laxatives: reduce straining during defecation.
  • Oral venotonics — Diosmin: improve venous tone and microcirculation and reduce bleeding and itching. The dose in an acute attack is 2–3 tablets/day for 5–7 days.
  • Topical anesthetics and decongestants: Lidocaine to relieve pain and itching — not exceeding one week (risk of mucosal thinning). Tribenoside + Lidocaine combination: ointment 3 times daily for up to 3 weeks.

Complementary treatments and education

  • Fiber supplements: 20–35 g/day + fluids 1.5–2 liters, reduce bleeding and recurrence (gradual effect over 6 weeks).
  • Sitz bath: soaking the area in warm water for 10–15 minutes 2–3 times daily, and adding Dead Sea salts relieves the spasm and pain and has an antiseptic effect.
  • Ring cushion: reduces pressure on the area, and avoid prolonged sitting.

🚩 Critical Cases & Referral Criteria (Red Flags)

When the pharmacist stops dispensing OTC and refers the patient to a physician immediately:

Heartburn / Ulcer — ALARM symptoms

  • Pain radiating to other areas of the body (cardiac CVS or gallbladder).
  • Pain on an empty stomach 30 minutes after eating (gastric ulcer).
  • Pain 2–3 hours after eating that wakes the patient at night (duodenal ulcer).
  • Persistent symptoms not responding to OTC and lifestyle change (two-week treatment).
  • Anemia: fatigue, pallor, shortness of breath.
  • Melena: blood in the stool (black) | Hematemesis: blood in the vomit.
  • Anorexia (loss of appetite) and Loss of weight.

Diarrhea

  • Bloody diarrhea or mucus in the stool (bacterial).
  • Undiagnosed chronic diarrhea (IBS / IBD).
  • Moderate to severe dehydration (needing IV fluids).
  • Children: persistence > 2–3 days, refusal to eat and drink, no response to ORS, or fever > 38°C for infants 0–36 months.

Constipation

  • Presence of blood (hemorrhoids, anal fissure, upper bleeding).
  • Weight loss, lethargy, cold intolerance (suspected hypothyroidism).
  • Chronic constipation > 14 days without a clear cause.

Hemorrhoids and other conditions

  • Long-standing hemorrhoid symptoms not previously examined by a physician and with which OTC has been tried.
  • Prolapsing tissue needing manual reduction or irreducible.
  • Ulcerative colitis (requires diagnosis and medical supervision).

Q1: A patient takes Clopidogrel (Plavix) and requests a heartburn treatment for two weeks. What is the most appropriate option?
a) Omeprazole 20mg
b) Pantoprazole 20mg
c) Esomeprazole 20mg
d) Rabeprazole 10mg
Answer: b) Pantoprazole. Omeprazole (and esomeprazole to a lesser degree) inhibits the CYP2C19 enzyme necessary to convert Plavix to its active form, reducing its antiplatelet activity. Pantoprazole inhibits this enzyme less and is safer with Plavix.
Q2: A mother of an 8-year-old with watery diarrhea for two days without fever or blood. What do you recommend first?
a) Loperamide
b) ORS solution
c) An antibiotic
d) Racecadotril only
Answer: b) ORS. It is the first-line for any age because dehydration is the most dangerous complication. Loperamide is prohibited under 12 years, and an antibiotic is not justified in watery diarrhea without signs of bacterial infection (fever/blood).
Q3: A patient whose diarrhea is accompanied by fever and bloody mucoid stool. What is the correct decision?
a) Dispense Loperamide
b) Dispense Kaolin adsorbents
c) Refer to a physician
d) ORS only and wait a week
Answer: c) Referral. Blood and mucus with fever indicate an invasive bacterial infection (Red Flag). Loperamide is prohibited here because it may worsen the infection by retaining the pathogen. The decision is referral (with the option of giving ORS as support).
Q4: A woman in the third trimester complains of constipation. What is the safest laxative?
a) Senna
b) Bisacodyl
c) Lactulose
d) Sodium Phosphate enema
Answer: c) Lactulose. Safe in pregnancy and lactation. Senna is avoided in pregnancy and lactation, and stimulants generally are not preferred. Bulk-forming laxatives, Lactulose, and Glycerin are safe options for the pregnant woman.
Q5: A patient requests Buscopan for abdominal cramps and has a history of glaucoma. What do you do?
a) Dispense it normally
b) Withhold it and suggest Mebeverine
c) Advise doubling the dose
d) Dispense eye drops with it
Answer: b) Mebeverine. Hyoscine (Buscopan) is an antimuscarinic contraindicated in glaucoma and prostate problems. Mebeverine is a safe alternative because it acts directly on the intestinal muscles with very low anticholinergic effects.
Q6: Why are proton pump inhibitors (PPIs) taken 30–60 minutes before the meal?
a) To reduce nausea
b) Because food reduces their bioavailability ~50% and to target the active pumps
c) To prevent constipation
d) To increase absorption in the colon
Answer: b). Food reduces bioavailability by about 50%, and taking them before the meal ensures an effective concentration when the proton pumps are activated with the meal for maximum acid inhibition.
Q7: A patient takes an antacid and another chronic drug. What is the essential advice?
a) Take them together
b) Separate them by at least two hours
c) Stop the chronic drug
d) Double the antacid dose
Answer: b). Antacids reduce the absorption of other drugs, so they are separated from any drug by at least two hours. The same principle applies to adsorbents (Kaolin) and activated charcoal (≥ 2 hours).
Q8: A pregnant woman suffers from morning sickness. Which option is suitable and safe (Category B)?
a) Meclizine + Pyridoxine (B6)
b) Loperamide
c) Bisacodyl
d) Hyoscine
Answer: a). Meclizine + B6 is suitable for nausea and vomiting of pregnancy (Category B). Likewise Ondansetron, Metoclopramide, and Domperidone are classified B. The other options are unrelated to nausea.
Q9: A patient complains of persistent heartburn for 3 weeks despite using Omeprazole, with weight loss. What is the decision?
a) Increase the PPI dose
b) Add an Antacid
c) Immediate referral to a physician
d) Switch to an H2 blocker
Answer: c) Referral. Non-response after two weeks + weight loss are alarm symptoms (ALARM). OTC must be stopped and referral made to rule out serious diseases.
Q10: Why is glucose an essential component in the ORS solution?
a) For sweetening only
b) A carrier for sodium (and with it water) across the intestinal mucosa + an energy source
c) To kill bacteria
d) To slow motility
Answer: b). Glucose acts as a carrier for sodium ions across the small intestine mucosa (and thereby water absorption), and provides the energy needed for this process.
Q11: A 4-month-old infant, viral diarrhea. Which antisecretory agent can be used?
a) Loperamide
b) Racecadotril (Hidrasec)
c) Senna
d) Nothing — always refer
Answer: b) Racecadotril. Approved for viral diarrhea in infants > 3 months (not exceeding 5 days). Loperamide is prohibited under 12 years. With the necessity of monitoring dehydration signs.
Q12: A patient uses Senna and asks about the change in urine color. How do you reassure him?
a) A danger sign that must be stopped
b) A normal effect — may color the urine yellow-brown to red depending on acidity
c) Evidence of bleeding
d) A drug allergy
Answer: b). Senna may color the urine a hue ranging from yellow-brown to red depending on its acidity, which is a known and unconcerning effect. But he is reminded that it is not intended for long-term use.
Q13: A diarrhea-predominant IBS patient (IBS-D). What is the appropriate treatment option for the predominant symptom?
a) Lactulose
b) Loperamide
c) Bisacodyl
d) High-dose Psyllium immediately
Answer: b) Loperamide. For IBS-D, initially 2–4 tablets/day maximum 6–8 (16mg), stopped if no improvement within 48 hours. The constipation type (IBS-C) is treated with soluble fiber. With Mebeverine for pain.
Q14: A hemorrhoid patient requests topical Lidocaine ointment. What is the advice about duration?
a) Continuous monthly use
b) Not exceeding one week to avoid mucosal thinning
c) Once only
d) Only with an antibiotic
Answer: b). The topical anesthetic Lidocaine is not used for more than a week due to the risk of mucosal thinning. Treatment is completed with venotonics (Diosmin), fiber, and a sitz bath.
Q15: A patient on Metformin complains of chronic diarrhea (> 14 days). What is the most important diagnostic step in the pharmacy?
a) Dispense adsorbents directly
b) Ask WWHAM about the medications and link the cause to metformin with referral assessment
c) Give an antibiotic
d) Long-term Loperamide
Answer: b). Chronic diarrhea (>14 days) is a referral criterion, and metformin is a known drug cause of diarrhea. The skilled pharmacist applies WWHAM (the M letter) to detect the drug cause, and refers if necessary rather than covering the symptom.
